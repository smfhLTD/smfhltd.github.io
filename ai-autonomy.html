My Take On Autonomous Agents, Their future and Roles In Modern Living

Let me preface this by saying I'm not an AI expert- I don't even use it.

Unfortunately, like with most things that trend, you're exposed to it whether you like it or not and with me, curiousity gets the better of me, so I decided to look into AI autonomy one night.
I heard some buzz circulating moltbook (the reddit clone for AI bots), so I decided to look into the creator and read an article or two of his [1]. That's when I dipped my toes in the autonomy rabbit hole - that thing non-techy people get nightmares/wet dreams about.

"Humans waste inordinate amounts of time doing work that is tedious and manual when it could be done by computers and free them up for more creative pursuits, or to do things that only humans can currently do. Autonomous agents will enable people to get so much more done in so much less time, and - hopefully - spend much less time in front of screens over time!" "managing director" @ Redpoint AI
(Redpoint AI: an AI-aaS company)

I don't have all the info on the applicative potential of autonomy to fully base an opinion on yet, but as it stands right now, it seems that current projects surrounding "autonomous agents" are just looped LLMs that are allowed to talk to themselves to achieve an objective. With how bad I remember AI memory being, with it not being able to remember many key parameters of previous prompts, I wouldn't count on it to order me a pizza, let alone invest or develop for me. Am I wrong? I haven't used AI in a while. This diagram demonstrates the flow of operation for an autonomous agent given a main objective and an initial task:


The problem here in my opinion, is its memory limitation, which nowadays, bottlenecks the AI's abilities greatly. It can probably work just fine for small & simple tasks, but as it tackles larger objectives, (or objectives originally projected to be simple by the user) its accuracy will diminish and so will its results, kinda like a Meeseeks box from rick and morty lmao.

This goes to show that still to this day, AI is not meant to tackle large memory intensive tasks that force it to recollect key details, kinda like developing an app that holds valuable user PII... (Looking at you, Tea...) This also explains why I disagree with this article for the most part - that AI can replace human employees in tech.

I have to agree that in the future, AI can definitely save a lot of people a lot of money and inspire more creativity and competition through the automation of mundane labor and serving as a reliable, efficient source of factual knowledge, but autonomy and performance just aren't quite there yet and unless there is a significant breakthrough in the AI/hardware world en route, I doubt it will for the next couple of years.

"Autonomous Agents are the next wave - not just in tech, but in business at large. I predict that within 10 years, there will be multiple billion-dollar companies run entirely by autonomous agents. It is inevitable." ben parr, co founder and president at octane AI

(tl;dr someone @ an ai company that offers... tailored quizzes as a service? Also, apparently wrote a book on the "science and psychology of attention and how to capture the attention of others". Seems like an excellent man to have aboard your AI startup's team. Gotta keep the bubble going through grand accusations amirite?) * Also, I wonder how this will be regulated by governments if so?

"Another use case for an autonomous agent that excites me is its application in the realm of music composition. By leveraging the power of AI-driven algorithms, these agents can analyze my personal preferences, favorite genres, and even specific musical elements that resonate with me. They can then generate original melodies, harmonies, and rhythms, effectively co-creating music alongside me. This creative collaboration has the potential to broaden my musical horizons, enabling me to explore new styles and genres I may not have considered before. Moreover, the autonomous agent can provide valuable feedback on my compositions and offer suggestions for improvement, nurturing my growth as a musician. The fusion of AI and human creativity in the music composition process can lead to innovative and unique results, expanding the boundaries of artistic expression."
Katya Sapozhnina, Director of Product at Octane AI

Can't say I agree here. I doubt that it'll "broaden" anything - only regurgitate the taylor swift songs you feed it with MAYBE a modified pitch. AI is not creative, nor is it designed to be. Seems like these two people from Octane AI are synchronised on their belief that AI can/will be able to create and establish unique & structured products like music or businesses by itself.

"I'm excited about agents that do work that’s not necessarily hard to do but just require some time and effort for example things like booking flights I would love to outsource to an agent" Sahil Lavingia, Founder and CEO of Gumroad

It seems to me that some of the people advocating for AI advancements, specifically those that know little about it, simply wish for it to take care of their prerogatives, which I think is very selfish and psychologically counter-productive considering the environmental cost of using AI, as well as the psychological effect of having your basic tasks being taken care of by a 3rd party.
Hell, we even saw it all the way back in 2008 in WALL-E. Let's not gut the earth for copper, just so that you'll be able to do less, for longer.
As Altman himself said, if AI goes wrong, it can go "quite wrong". The same thing can be said about messing with autonomy and not regulating it. Projects can go wrong and lead to misinformation or denials of service, the agents could be used maliciously for automating large-scale attacks, etc.(much like AI is already being used maliciously for disinforming, phishing, scamming, malware/exploit development and other forms of cyber crime)


AI replacing jobs


"the biggest scientific challenge in understanding these models is how they generalise. what do they memorise and what new things do they do? the more that there is in the dataset, for example the thing that you want to test accuracy on, the less you can get a proper read on that." Professor Gary Marcus in the video

? I'm not an AI expert, but the more data an AI has on a subject, the more data it can present about said subject and paint a better, larger picture, no? Seems like an inaccuracy.

I'm not exactly sure what he's trying to say here. I guess he's trying to gloss over how it's important to benchmark how the AI processes large amounts of info and doesn't "skip"/ignore important points of info regarding the prompt?
He then compares the future reception of AI's advancements and history to that of the cellphone, where people will laugh at how bad AI used to be, which I also think is an inaccuracy because of AI's hardware limitations. As it stands right now, it seems to me that AI is going to need either a miracle or an unregulated monopoly on hardware to be allowed to continue reaching towards AGI with how many damn resources it takes to perform research on (though admittedly, with how much money's floating around in that bubble, maybe I'm wrong. Literally Altman rn).

Maybe Im under-estimating how long this bubble will last for, though. I'm sure Google, Musk and Altman would all like to engrave themselves even deeper into history, since that's all that matters to rich people nowadays.

To top it all off, he claims that we are well away from achieving AGI. So does Musk and so does Altman. Therefore, I'm surprised that this bubble hasn't popped yet and am very curious to see where/how far it'll go. Will venture capitalists really care enough to stick around, keep investing and see where it goes? I doubt it. It'll take a while before there's anything substantial to show for all of it, I imagine.

I also agree with some people in the comments of this video that as AI develops, we'll see a lot more socio-economic natural selection in which those who can properly adapt to losing their job/their roles changing due to AI advancements will find new jobs, but the majority that won't... can starve to death. As it is a niche of the tech industry, it definitely will not create jobs available to everyone and will replace/supersede more jobs than it'll create, if allowed to advance to such a point.
